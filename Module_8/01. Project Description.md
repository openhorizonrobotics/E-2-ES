## Chapter 1: Project Description

If you've worked through the earlier modules of this course, you've spent a fair amount of time wiring the STM32F4 Discovery board up to one sensor or another. And if you've done that, you already know the frustration.

The Discovery board is a capable piece of hardware, but it's genuinely awkward to work with on a bench. Its GPIO is broken out as dual rows of male headers, with the pin labels printed on the opposite side of the board relative to the pins themselves. In practice, that means you're constantly flipping the board over to read a label, then flipping it back to make a connection -- with the kind of precision that starts to feel less like electronics and more like microsurgery. And when you're done, the board sits suspended in mid-air on a tangle of jumper wires, with more wires disappearing underneath it. It's messy, fragile, and easy to get wrong.

There's also no on-board USB-UART bridge. Unlike an Arduino, which handles serial debugging over the same USB cable you use for power, the Discovery board requires a separate FTDI adapter and its own set of connections. That's one more thing to wire up, one more thing to knock loose, and one more source of confusion when something isn't working.

I got tired of it, and decided to do something about it.

The result is the **STM32 Integration Board** -- a custom PCB designed to make the Discovery board significantly more pleasant to work with, while also bringing together every peripheral from Module 5 onto a single, self-contained platform.

### What the Board Does

At its core, the Integration Board accepts the STM32F4 Discovery as a plug-in module via female headers. Those headers fan out every GPIO connection to clearly labeled male pin rows on the surface of the board -- no more flipping, no more guessing. An FTDI USB-UART breakout is also slotted directly into the board, so serial debugging is available the moment you power up.

Beyond the Discovery itself, the board integrates five additional modules, all of which you'll recognise from earlier in the course:

- **MPU6050**: 6-axis IMU over I2C
- **VL53L1 ToF**: Time-of-flight distance sensor over I2C
- **PCA9685**: 16-channel PWM driver over I2C, for servos or LEDs
- **A4988**: Stepper motor driver
- **FTDI USB-UART Bridge**: For serial debugging independent of the Discovery's ST-Link

All five are breakout boards, seated in female headers and fanned out from the Discovery's GPIO. Nothing here was designed from scratch at the silicon level -- the goal was integration, not reinvention.

### What You'll Take Away From This Module

In this module, I've demonstrated how to take a real hardware problem -- a messy, friction-filled development setup -- and solve it properly, by designing a PCB from scratch. That journey goes from an idea on paper, through a schematic, through a routed board layout, and finally to a physical, assembled piece of hardware.

Along the way, you'll learn how to translate a circuit idea into a schematic, how to move from a schematic to a board layout, and how to think through the planning problems that come up at each stage -- things like power distribution, connector placement, and making sure the physical reality of the board matches the logical intent of the schematic.

You need to learn this because it makes you a more complete robotics engineer. The teams and companies you'll want to work with don't just need people who can write firmware, they appreciate candidates who can also sit down and design hardware when the situation calls for it. The ability to do both makes you genuinely self-sufficient. You don't have to wait on someone else to build the thing you need. You can just build it.
